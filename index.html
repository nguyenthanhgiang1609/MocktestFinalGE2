Reading Passage 1 — Supermarket Secrets

Have you ever noticed that milk and eggs are almost always at the back of the supermarket? This is not an accident. Supermarkets use many clever tricks to make shoppers spend more money than they planned. Once you know these tricks, you can become a smarter shopper.

One of the most common tricks is placing everyday items like milk, bread, and eggs far from the entrance. When shoppers go to get these items, they have to walk through many aisles. Along the way, they often see other products and put extra things in their basket — things they did not plan to buy. This is exactly what the supermarket wants.

Supermarkets also use music and lighting to influence shoppers. Bright lights in the fruit and vegetable section make the food look fresh, colourful, and attractive. Slow, relaxing music makes people walk more slowly and spend more time in the store. Research shows that supermarkets with slow background music can sell up to 38% more than stores with no music at all.

Another trick is called 'charm pricing'. A product that costs $9.99 feels much cheaper than one that costs $10.00, even though the difference is only one cent. Shoppers often do not notice this small difference, but it has a big effect on how much they spend.

Finally, supermarkets think carefully about where to place products on the shelves. The most expensive brands are always placed at eye level, where shoppers can see them easily. Cheaper products are put on the top or bottom shelves, where people are less likely to look. Children's snacks and toys are placed low on the shelves so that children can see them and ask their parents to buy them. By understanding these tricks, you can make smarter choices every time you go shopping.

Reading Passage 2 — Art for Everyone

When most people think of art, they think of paintings hanging in museums or sculptures inside expensive galleries. But public art is different. It is art that you can see and enjoy outside, in parks, on streets, or on the walls of buildings. You do not need a ticket or an appointment. Public art belongs to everyone.

One well-known example of public art is the work of Jim Denevan, an American artist. Denevan is famous for making very large drawings in sand on beaches and in deserts. He uses only a long stick and his feet to create his patterns. His drawings are enormous — sometimes they are several kilometres wide and take him many days to finish. But his art does not last long. When the sea comes in, the waves wash the drawings away completely. Denevan says this is not a problem. For him, the temporary nature of his art makes it more beautiful and more meaningful.

Another exciting example is the work of Thomas Dambo, a Danish artist who loves the environment. Dambo builds giant wooden figures called trolls and places them in forests and parks near cities. His trolls are sometimes five or six metres tall. What makes his work special is that he builds them entirely from recycled wood — old doors, broken furniture, and pieces of wood that other people throw away. Each troll also has a short folk story written near it, which encourages visitors to think about nature and the importance of looking after our planet.

Both of these artists remind us that great art does not need to be in a museum, and it does not need to cost a lot of money. Sometimes the most powerful art is the kind that is free, unexpected, and open for everyone to enjoy.
